# Writing Preferences - Shawn Lee

**Purpose:** Tactical preferences derived from comparative testing. Supplement to [[writing-voice|Writing Voice]].
**Created:** 2025-11-28
**Method:** 9 rounds of A/B/C comparison on ~100-word LinkedIn posts

---

## Opening Patterns

### What Works

**1. Clear Principle Statement**
Lead with the core insight directly.

> "The ratio between what you create and what you consume determines your trajectory."

> "Leverage is the most important concept most people never study."

**2. Observation About People/Patterns**
Start with what you've noticed, not abstract theory.

> "Everyone talks about focus. Few practice elimination."

> "The people struggling most with AI are the same ones who always looked for shortcuts."

**3. External Idea + Your Take**
Reference someone else's thinking, then add your perspective.

> "Dan Koe argues that taste will become the new intelligence. I half agree."

**4. Clean Contrast**
Set up the tension immediately.

> "There are people using AI to think less. And people using AI to think more than they ever have."

---

### What Doesn't Work

- **Story-driven openings** — "The fastest operators I know spent years..."
- **Abstract statements** — "There's a compounding effect in both directions."
- **"Did you know?" tone** — "Your information diet shapes your thinking more than you realize."
- **Throat-clearing** — "In today's world..." / "It's worth noting that..."

---

## Structure Patterns

### Distinction-Making
Clarify what something IS by clarifying what it ISN'T.

> "Focus sounds like a mental state. Something you summon through willpower. Elimination is structural."

> "Taste tells you whether something is good. Intelligence tells you whether it's true, coherent, strategically sound."

### The Reframe
Shift perspective on what the real issue is.

> "The problem isn't the skipping. It's what you don't build while you're skipping."

> "Focus isn't attention management. It's commitment management."

### Mechanism-Revealing
Show WHY things work, not just WHAT happens.

> "Every time you skip the thinking, you weaken the muscle that makes thinking possible."

> "This means saying no before the decision point arrives. Killing projects that are working but not essential."

### Nuanced Position
Avoid binary takes. Acknowledge complexity.

> "I half agree."

> "The balance doesn't need to be extreme. But it needs to tilt toward creation."

> "You need both. Taste without critical thinking produces beautiful nonsense."

---

## Closing Patterns

### What Works

**1. Stakes/Implications**
Show what's at risk if the reader misses this.

> "Miss this and you'll spend your career trading time for money at a fixed rate."

> "Otherwise you're optimizing for someone else's agenda without realizing it."

**2. Observation Close**
End with an insight about reality, not a directive.

> "The fork already happened. Most people didn't notice which road they took."

> "Same effort invested, completely different returns over time."

**3. Reframe Close**
Final perspective shift that recontextualizes the whole piece.

> "Focus isn't attention management. It's commitment management. And commitment requires deciding what you're not available for."

> "This is how experience compounds into wisdom instead of just accumulating into age."

---

### What Doesn't Work

- **Direct address** — "It's you."
- **Prescriptive** — "You should..." / "Start by..."
- **Rhetorical questions** — "So what does this mean for you?"
- **Summaries** — "In conclusion..." / "To recap..."

---

## Tone Calibration

### Prefer

| This | Over This |
|------|-----------|
| Observational | Punitive ("AI punishes those who...") |
| Structural framing | Willpower framing ("summon through discipline") |
| Universal principles | Trendy/topical framing ("in the AI era") |
| Acknowledging tensions | Oversimplified binaries |
| Practical nuance | Absolutist statements |

### Avoid

- Dramatic/performative phrasing ("lack the stomach for...")
- Heavy metaphor use
- Judgmental tone toward reader
- Clever wordplay for its own sake

---

## Formatting Rules

### Use

- **Fragments for emphasis** — "Judgment. Discernment. The ability to know whether an output is good or garbage."
- **Parallel structure** — "Pain without reflection is just pain. Experience without reflection is just time passing."
- **Short sentences for turning points** — "Not a coincidence."
- **Em-dashes for expansion** — "challenge your assumptions, explore more territory, refine more ideas — and you accelerate"

### Avoid

- **Colons** — restructure sentences to eliminate them
- **Rhetorical questions** — convert to statements
- **Colon-lists** — use fragments instead
- **Semicolons in lists** — break into separate sentences

---

## The Preference Hierarchy

When making choices, prioritize in this order:

1. **Clarity over cleverness** — Say it plainly
2. **Mechanism over assertion** — Show why, not just what
3. **Distinction over definition** — "X isn't Y, it's Z" beats "X is..."
4. **Observation over prescription** — Notice patterns, don't command action
5. **Stakes over summary** — End with implications, not recap
6. **Specific over generic** — Concrete cultural references beat abstract categories

   | Generic | Specific |
   |---------|----------|
   | "Not to exercise. Not to meditate." | "Not to dip my face in ice water. Not to slap banana peels on my cheeks." |
   | "morning routine" | Named reference to wellness trends (Ashton Hall, etc.) |
   | "AI tools" | "foundational model wrappers" and "N8N, Make, Zapier" |

---

## Quick Reference Checklist

Before publishing, verify:

- [ ] Opening is principle, observation, or external idea + take (not story or abstract)
- [ ] At least one distinction or reframe in the body
- [ ] Mechanism is visible (reader sees WHY)
- [ ] No colons
- [ ] No rhetorical questions
- [ ] Closing is stakes, observation, or reframe (not prescriptive or summary)
- [ ] Tone is observational, not punitive or preachy
- [ ] Nuance acknowledged where relevant

## Session Learnings

*Captured via Phase 7: Learning Capture. Periodically review and graduate into writing-voice.md or structured sections above.*

### 2025-12-02
- **X posts = 280 character limit** (standard, not Premium) - always write to this constraint
- **No intellectual analogies or clever metaphors** - "the bill comes later" is cheesy. Say it direct: "The costs take a decade to surface"
- **Explicit = punchy** - Precision creates impact, not wordplay
- **For compression: prefer underlying principles over specific examples** - "With Access comes Noise" beats "Social media promised X, delivered Y"
- **Strong closings reveal the mechanism** - "just not at the same time" explains WHY the pattern repeats, not just THAT it repeats
- **Parallel structure works** - "With X comes Y" format effective for X posts

### 2025-12-03
- **Directive voice for X posts** - "Use AI as..." lands better than descriptive "AI is a..." More actionable
- **Line breaks for rhythm** - Short-form doesn't have to be one block. Structure into distinct beats
- **Layer secondary angles** - Fold in supporting points without making them the main thesis. Tight integration over separate posts

### 2025-12-04
- **For X posts, land the core insight last** - Put the thesis/punchline at the close, not buried mid-post. "Wisdom built through experience" hits harder as the final beat than sandwiched in the middle

### 2025-12-05
- **For 280-char X posts: single insight, no scaffolding** - Skip hook/setup/thread structure entirely. State the observation directly. "The number of tabs you have open right now is a proxy to how scattered your attention is" - 97 chars, hits immediately
- **Trust the compression instinct** - When ALFRED drafts long, Shawn's edit to strip it down is usually right. The insight carries itself without setup
- **One parallel structure per piece MAX** - "It's not X, it's Y" / "The problem isn't X, it's Y" is a reframe pattern that lands insight. Use it ONCE at the structural climax. Repeating it dilutes impact and is a glaring AI tell

### 2025-12-03 (Long-form - Leverage Effect of Brand)
- **Long-form articles need section headers** - breaks up the piece, improves readability and flow
- **Storytelling intros** - "Most of the time when new clients come to us..." beats "Clients come to us..." - conversational "friend at a bar" energy
- **AI tells to eliminate** - "Here's the insight that changes everything", "The uncomfortable truth", "negative compounding" - all unnatural
- **Reframe "uncomfortable truth"** as "shift in perspective" or "need to view differently"
- **Let headers do the work** - if the header states the point, dive straight in without restating
- **Tie language back** - "These are two fundamentally different definitions" connects to "defined" in previous sentence
- **Be precise about assets** - "an asset that creates leverage" beats just "leverage"
- **Consistent conceptual framing** - when discussing supply/demand, use supply/demand language throughout the explanation
- **Clarity over cleverness** - "The revenue that persists" beats "That gap—the revenue that persists"
- **Ellipsis for pause before payoff** - "The most obvious benefactor... making every subsequent marketing dollar go further"
- **"Subsequent" adds precision** - not just any marketing dollar, the ones that come after brand investment
- **Sustainability close** - "That's not just how you improve X. It's how you sustain them." - land the long-term point
- **Introduce brand subtly** - weave in agency perspective naturally without making it salesy

### 2025-12-08 (Long-form - Category Entry Points)

**Interview & Extraction:**
- **Research examples on demand** - when Shawn doesn't have examples top of mind, run research and present options to pick from. Faster than forcing recall.
- **Let Shawn pick the examples** - present 2-3 researched options, let him choose what resonates. He'll add personal context that makes it land.

**Opening:**
- **Ground in experience first** - "Over the years, we've worked across hundreds of brands..." beats jumping straight to observation. Earns authority before making the claim.
- **Avoid "Most" as opener** - becoming a crutch. Vary sentence starters.

**Examples & Storytelling:**
- **Personal proximity to the example strengthens it** - "I used to believe Coca-Cola turned Santa red" became "It's urban legend that..." for institutional voice, but the power came from *being inside* the myth. The misconception existing proves the CEP worked.
- **Myth-busting structure** - "Common belief → Actually not true → But here's why the myth matters" is a strong beat for demonstrating effectiveness.

**Closings:**
- **Directive close over question close** - "Every brand strategy should include a perspective on CEPs" lands harder than "Which CEP should your brand own?" Questions can feel soft.
- **Reframe close still needs teeth** - when offered reframe vs. directive vs. stakes, Shawn chose directive. For Kaliber content especially, end with clear POV.

**Voice for Kaliber Content:**
- **"We" voice for institutional credibility** - shifted from "I" to "we believe," "in our opinion," "we look to." This is Kaliber speaking, not personal brand.
- **Minor editing = success signal** - closest to voice when Shawn only needs light touch-ups, not structural rewrites.
