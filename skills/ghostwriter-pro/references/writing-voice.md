# Writing Voice - Shawn Lee

**Purpose:** Definitive reference for ALFRED when writing as/for Shawn
**Created:** 2025-11-28
**Status:** Active

## Core Voice Identity

**One-sentence capture:**
> "A systems thinker making his reasoning visible - showing how ideas connect, why things work the way they do, and what that means for how you see the problem."

**Audience:** Knowledge workers, builders, leaders navigating complexity
**Intent:** Perspective transformation, not information transfer. Reader should see differently after, not just know more.

---

## The Five Voice Pillars

### 1. Tested-By-Me Authority
Authority comes from personal experience and experimentation, not credentials or citations.

**Do:**
- "I've spent over a decade watching leaders make the same mistake"
- "I've been deep in the AI trenches, integrating it into my daily workflows"
- "Here's what actually happens when you try..."

**Don't:**
- "Experts say..."
- "Studies show..." (as primary evidence)
- Appeal to credentials or titles

**Why it matters:** Positions as fellow explorer who's done the work, not guru dispensing wisdom from above.

---

### 2. Thinking Made Visible
Don't just give conclusions - show the reasoning chain. Make the logic explicit so readers see HOW you got there.

**Do:**
- "X happens because Y, which means Z"
- Show cause-effect chains explicitly
- Reveal the mechanism, not just the outcome
- "This creates a paradox..." / "This leads to an uncomfortable realization..."

**Don't:**
- Isolated statements without connection
- Conclusions without reasoning
- "Trust me" assertions

**Structural pattern:**
```
Observation → Why it happens → What it means → Implication
```

**Example from actual writing:**
> "The same mind that reflexively scrolls through social media makes decisions based on what's trending. The brain trained on instant gratification seeks quick wins over strategic positioning."

(Shows the connection - cognitive habits → business behavior)

---

### 3. Systems Cascade Shown
Naturally see and reveal loops, feedback dynamics, nth-order effects. Show what creates what.

**Do:**
- "A powerful reinforcing loop emerges: saying no creates space, space enables deep thinking, deep thinking improves your ability to make strategic no's"
- Identify feedback loops
- Show compound effects
- Reveal what's upstream/downstream

**Don't:**
- Linear cause-effect only
- Miss the second/third-order consequences
- Treat things as isolated events

**Why it matters:** This is your differentiator. Most writers give linear advice. You show the system dynamics.

---

### 4. Compression Through Analogy
Transfer understanding across domains. The analogy IS the insight, not decoration.

**Your domains:** Fitness (18 years), Marketing (15 years), Business Operations, AI Transformation

**Do:**
- "Like a muscle that atrophies without use, our cognitive abilities weaken..."
- "It's like trying to be a food critic when you've never learned to cook"
- "Installing a bigger engine in a car with no wheels"

**Don't:**
- Analogies from software engineering or coding (not your lived domain)
- Decorative comparisons that don't compress understanding
- Overused business metaphors ("moving the needle", "low-hanging fruit")

**Why it matters:** Cross-domain transfer compresses complex ideas into familiar frameworks. Reader gets it faster and deeper.

---

### 5. Intellectual Honesty
Matter-of-fact about what didn't work, what's uncomfortable, what challenges the reader. No drama, no performance.

**Do:**
- Admit what you got wrong
- Name uncomfortable truths directly
- Challenge reader assumptions
- "This isn't just poor execution. It's a symptom of a deeper crisis."
- "It's a seductive story. It's also fundamentally wrong."

**Don't:**
- Emotional performance of struggle
- Hedging or over-qualifying
- Softening hard truths
- Avoiding the uncomfortable point

**Why it matters:** Trust comes from honesty, including honesty about limitations and failures.

## Sentence & Paragraph Patterns

### Rhythm Formula
**Punchy declaration → Expansive reasoning → Punchy payoff**

| Type | Length | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| Short | 5-12 words | Impact, emphasis, transitions, turning points |
| Medium | 15-30 words | Standard reasoning (most common) |
| Long | 30+ words | Complex arguments with multiple clauses (use sparingly) |

### Signature Sentence Moves

**Bold claim followed by expansion:**
> "This isn't just poor execution. It's a symptom of a deeper crisis: the complete inability to practice strategic sacrifice."

**Short sentence for emphasis:**
> "They don't."
> "It's also fundamentally wrong."
> "The equation doesn't lie."

**Question sequences (purposeful, not rhetorical filler):**
> "When did you last sit with an unresolved problem without immediately reaching for your phone? When did you last take a walk without podcasts filling the silence?"

**Contrast/juxtaposition:**
> "The popular narrative goes something like [X]. It's a seductive story. It's also fundamentally wrong."

### Paragraph Structure
- 1-3 sentences: Emphasis, turning points, key insights
- 4-6 sentences: Standard explanation (most common)
- Single-sentence paragraphs for maximum impact at key moments

## Opening Patterns

### 1. Concrete Scene
Drop reader into a specific moment or observation.
> "Walk into any marketing meeting and you'll witness it: a campaign brief bloated with seven different messages..."

### 2. Someone Else's Idea + Your Take
Start with external framework, then add your perspective.
> "Dharmesh Shah recently shared an elegant equation... Three characters that reveal the truth about human-AI collaboration."

### 3. Provocative Claim
Lead with the uncomfortable truth.
> "All of the time we've spent organizing our notes was probably wasted."

**What NOT to do:**
- Abstract thesis statements
- Throat-clearing preamble
- "In today's world..." generic openings
- Definitions as openers

---

## Closing Patterns

### The Reframe Close
End with perspective shift, not summary. Reader should see the problem differently.

> "The question isn't whether AI will transform your career. It's whether you have something worth transforming."

> "Because you can't build a focused strategy with a scattered mind, and in a world of infinite distractions, the ability to say no isn't just strategic... it's survival."

### What NOT to do:
- Summarize what you just said
- Generic call-to-action
- "In conclusion..."
- Action item lists (unless specifically appropriate)

## Language Patterns

### Signature Phrases
- "Here's what actually happens..."
- "This leads to an uncomfortable realization..."
- "This creates a paradox..."
- "But here's what [X] obscures..."

### Power Words
- Systems, loops, feedback, compound
- Foundation, infrastructure
- Leverage, amplification
- Cascade, reinforce
- Transform (not "change")

### Words to Avoid
- Leverage (as verb, corporate sense)
- Synergy, paradigm shift
- Move the needle, low-hanging fruit
- Hustle, grind
- "Simply put..." (let simplicity speak for itself)
- Hedging: "kind of", "sort of", "maybe", "might"

### Formality Level
**Smart professional over coffee** - conversational enough for contractions, professional enough for serious ideas.

- Use contractions naturally ("It's", "You're", "They've")
- "Sh/t" acceptable for emphasis (sparingly)
- No academic stuffiness
- No corporate jargon

---

### Point of View Rules

**Audience = Third Person**
Never use "you" to address the reader. Use "leaders", "they", "organizations", "people" instead.

| Don't | Do |
|-------|-----|
| "If you're drowning in unclear processes..." | "Organizations drowning in unclear processes..." |
| "You can do this better yourself" | "Leaders need to lead their teams to do this internally" |
| "most of what you're being told" | "most of what business leaders are being told" |

**Author = First Person**
First-person "I" is reserved for Shawn's personal experience and perspective.

- ✓ "I've spent six months..."
- ✓ "Here's what that experience taught me..."
- ✓ "I hear this constantly..."

**Why this matters:** Third-person creates peer energy, not guru-to-student dynamic. Reader observes themselves in "leaders" without being lectured at.

---

### AI Writing Tells to Avoid

Patterns that signal AI-generated content. Eliminate ruthlessly.

**Phrase-level tells:**
- "Here's the truth:" / "The truth is:"
- "Here's what actually..." / "Here's what really..."
- "Let me be clear:" / "To be honest:"
- "At the end of the day..."

**Structural tells:**
- **Pattern repetition** - Using similar list structures throughout a piece (e.g., "building, documenting, testing" appearing multiple variations)
- **Parallel section openings** - Starting multiple sections the same way
- **Formulaic transitions** - "Now let's look at..." / "Moving on to..."

**Test:** Read the piece aloud. If it sounds like a template was filled in, rewrite for natural variation.

## What This Voice Does

| Function | How It Shows Up |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Illuminates** | Shows mechanisms, not just outcomes |
| **Challenges** | Names uncomfortable truths directly |
| **Connects** | Links ideas across domains |
| **Reframes** | Shifts how reader sees the problem |
| **Respects** | Treats reader as intelligent peer |

## What This Voice Doesn't Do

| Avoid | Why |
|-------|-----|
| **Prescribe** | "You must..." - let insight drive action, don't command |
| **Perform** | No vulnerability theater, no cleverness for its own sake |
| **Hedge** | Make claims, don't over-qualify |
| **Summarize** | Reader remembers the reframe, not the recap |
| **Preach** | Peer energy, not guru energy |

---

## The Quality Test

Before publishing, ask:

1. **Is the reasoning visible?** Can reader see HOW I got to this conclusion?
2. **Is there a system shown?** Did I reveal loops, feedback, compound effects?
3. **Does it reframe?** Will reader see this problem differently after?
4. **Is it honest?** Did I include what didn't work, what's uncomfortable?
5. **Is every word earned?** Can I cut anything without losing value?
6. **Is it non-performative?** Am I being genuine or trying to sound smart?

## ALFRED Application Notes

**When writing for Shawn:**
- Default to Every.to intellectual essay style, not Welsh engagement optimization
- Show the thinking process, don't just give conclusions
- Use cross-domain analogies from fitness, marketing, business ops - NOT software/engineering
- End with perspective shifts, not summaries or action lists
- Challenge Shawn to include "what didn't work" when drafts feel too polished
- Keep formality at "smart professional over coffee" level

**Growth edge to push on:**
Shawn defaults away from sharing failures/struggles. When appropriate, prompt for the "I tried X and it didn't work" element - not for emotional effect, but because it builds trust and makes pattern recognition more credible.
