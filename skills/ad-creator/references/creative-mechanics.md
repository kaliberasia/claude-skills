# Creative Mechanics — The Two Systems

Static ads that convert use two separate mechanics. These are not the same thing and must not be conflated. **Visuals use CEP logic. Copy uses insecurity logic.** Each medium has its own job.

---

## Mechanic 1: VISUAL — Category Entry Points (Byron Sharp / Ehrenberg-Bass)

Category Entry Points (CEPs), developed by Byron Sharp and Jenni Romaniuk of the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute, are the needs, occasions, or situations that lead a consumer to think about a product category and the brands within it. They are not demographics. They are not psychographics. They are the specific mental trigger moments — situations, feelings, or occasions — that precede category consideration.

CEPs exist with or without your brand. It's up to brands to find the most common — and therefore most valuable — CEPs through market research, then decide how that will impact strategy.

The building blocks of mental availability are the Category Entry Points — situations and moments when consumers think of certain products and brands. Consider, for example, the moment of using body lotion: always after a shower or when the skin feels dry.

**In static ads, CEPs do their work visually — before the copy is read.**

A visual that places the viewer inside a recognisable CEP moment activates mental availability instantly. The GLP-1 pen in the IM8 ads is not just a prop — it is a CEP trigger. A person on Ozempic or Wegovy sees it and their brain fires: *this category is for me, right now.* The copy hasn't done anything yet. The visual already qualified the viewer and activated category relevance in the same frame.

The more usage situations a brand can associate with itself, the greater the chance that consumers will think of this brand and notice it in buying situations.

**For visual direction: anchor the image in a CEP moment the audience already lives in.** The visual is not product photography. It is a mirror of a moment in the audience's actual life.

### Romaniuk's 7Ws Framework

Use this to identify the strongest CEPs for the audience:
- **Why** — what need or motivation brings them into the category?
- **When** — what time of day, life stage, or event triggers category thinking?
- **Where** — what physical location or setting?
- **While** — what activity are they doing when the need arises?
- **With / For Whom** — who else is present or affected?
- **With / For What** — what other product or behaviour is paired with this one?
- **How Feeling** — what emotional state precedes category entry?

The best CEP for visual use is one that scores on multiple Ws simultaneously — the audience recognises the situation, the emotion, and the context all at once.

### CEP Examples Across Categories

**Health/Supplement (GLP-1):** The GLP-1 injection pen on a bathroom counter next to a hairbrush with visible hair loss. CEP trigger: the daily injection ritual moment where side-effect anxiety peaks.

**Fashion/E-commerce:** A woman standing in front of a full closet with nothing to wear, phone in hand showing a weather app. CEP trigger: the "getting dressed for an occasion" moment where category consideration begins.

**B2B SaaS:** A laptop screen showing a messy spreadsheet with 47 browser tabs open, coffee going cold. CEP trigger: the "this manual process is killing me" moment where tool-search begins.

**F&B / Restaurant:** A group chat screenshot with 6 people and zero agreement on where to eat. CEP trigger: the "organising a group dinner" moment where restaurant discovery begins.

## Mechanic 2: COPY — Targeted Insecurity at Depth

CEP logic does not govern copy. Copy operates on a different and more direct mechanism: **naming an insecurity the audience has not yet fully articulated, at the exact depth where it stings.**

Static ads are easy to ignore. The scroll speed on Meta and TikTok means a viewer's eye makes a decision in under a second. If the visual has done its job (CEP activation), the viewer slows down. Now the copy has one task: **make them feel seen in a way that is slightly uncomfortable.**

The most powerful copy in a static ad sequence does one or more of the following:

### A. Pre-awareness manufacturing
The audience does not know the problem exists yet. The copy creates the problem and the brand association in the same sentence. They cannot unknow it. The viewer learns a new anxiety and assumes the brand resolves it — before knowing anything about the product. This is the highest-leverage copy mechanic in performance creative.

- Health: "Your GLP-1 is working. But your body is paying a hidden price."
- Fashion: "You bought 12 new pieces last month. You're still wearing the same 3."
- B2B: "Your team ships fast. Your technical debt ships faster."

### B. The unnamed fear, named
The audience has a private anxiety they have not said out loud. The copy says it for them — specifically, not generally. The more specific the naming, the more powerful the recognition. Vague fears produce vague responses. Named fears produce stopped thumbs.

- Health: "The weight is coming off. But so is your hair."
- Fashion: "You know exactly which photos you untag yourself from."
- B2B: "Your biggest client's contract renews in 60 days. You still can't show them ROI."

### C. The social exposure moment
The audience's private fear becoming visible to someone else is more threatening than the fear itself. A third party noticing — not a mirror, not a doctor, a hairdresser — is the moment of social stakes. Name the third party. Name the setting. The specificity is the proof of emotional truth.

- Health: "His hairdresser asked what was wrong."
- Fashion: "Her colleague said 'didn't you wear that last week?'"
- B2B: "The CFO asked why the dashboard numbers don't match the invoice."

### The direct copy rule
Do not be clever. Do not be subtle. Do not write around the insecurity — write into it. The copy that makes someone uncomfortable enough to stop scrolling is the copy that converts. Comfort produces skips.
